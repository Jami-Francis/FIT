The Gateway Router

A Gateway Router is a single device combining a modem (for DSL, cable, or fiber service) and a router (usually with Wi-Fi) and does not need an additional device to connect to the Internet.

A major benefit is fewer cords and less space than using two separate devices. If your Internet Service Provider (ISP) is charging a monthly fee, it’s often cheaper to buy your own and return the ISP-provided gateway.

The downside is that if the gateway router stops working, you will completely lose your Internet connection. Conversely, if only a separate router fails, you can remove it and connect directly into the modem to get online with at least one computer.

Additionally, gateway routers provided by ISPs are often older, refurbished models. This means the Wi-Fi may be based on older standards (like 2.4 GHz, which is slower than modern 5 GHz Wi-Fi 5 or Wi-Fi 6 over short distances). These older devices often come with basic security settings (like NAT/SPI) that are inferior to a dedicated, high-quality aftermarket router.

Some ISPs (like Zito Media) serving fiber optic lines are replacing Gateway Routers with an ONT (Optical Network Terminal), which offers better throughput but often lacks additional Ethernet ports or VOIP.

Remember, there is no problem mixing Windows, Apple, and Android products on any router, including network printers. Feel free to call us for recommendations on how to set up and protect your online connection."
