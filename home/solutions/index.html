Solutions

The first line of defense is ensuring your computer's operating system (OS) is completely up to date. Check Microsoft's Security and Critical Updates or Apple's Software Updates. Make sure your device is plugged into a charger before starting an update.

Both modern Macs (Tahoe 2025, Sequoia '24, Sonoma'23, Ventura '22) and Windows 11 PCs feature excellent built-in anti-malware and antivirus protection right out of the box. Take a moment to visit the 'Security' panel on your device to familiarize yourself with it. Next, determine if you actually need an additional third-party antivirus program. Most standard home systems do not. However, if children or grandchildren frequently use the machine, you might consider supplemental protection.

It is important to mention that Francis IT often discovers outdated, lapsed antivirus software lingering on client computers. Users see a security icon and assume they are safe, not realizing the application stopped updating when the annual subscription expired. By completely uninstalling an old, unsupported security app and rebooting, you allow the native operating system to immediately reclaim "command and control" of your system's  security and begin real-time protection.

It's equally crucial to keep Adobe Acrobat Reader updated to the latest version to ensure you have the latest vulnerability patches. Despite enhanced security measures built into modern PDF readers, always remain cautious when opening PDF attachments from unknown or untrusted sources.

By default, Acrobat Reader installs with auto-updates enabled. Never let an aggressive scareware pop-up on your web browser trick you into "updating" ANY application. Always perform software updates directly within the application itself and verify that Auto-Update is enabled. Getting tricked through malicious social engineering into upgrading or installing a fake application is the single most common vector for malware infections. (Learn more in our Article on Malware and read up on Social Engineering tactics.)

Internet Connectivity and Network Security

Now that your operating system and software are fully updated, the next consideration is how you connect to the internet.

If you are utilizing a standard gateway router provided by your Internet Service Provider (ISP), it relies on Network Address Translation (NAT) to manage your internal connection. However, the built-in hardware firewall features, such as Stateful Packet Inspection (SPI), are occasionally disabled or misconfigured out of the box. Because these physical gateway units are frequently refurbished and passed from customer to customer, they are rarely audited by cable companies between deployments. Asking your deployment technician to confirm that your hardware firewall and SPI are fully active is always a smart preventative step. If you are technically inclined, you can verify these settings yourself by logging into your router configuration page or calling your ISP support line.

If you suspect an active infection or invasive program is already present on your machine, you can run a targeted system scan using trusted retail or reputable online utility software. The curated web links located in our Online Help resource section can assist you in isolating the issue or confirming your system is entirely clean. Please strictly follow the software manufacturer's directions. Note that deeply embedded malware rarely clears up with a single generic scan, particularly on systems suffering from multiple overlapping infections.  Most importantly: do not fall for malicious browser advertisements claiming your system is compromised, which try to panic you into downloading a "cleanup tool" that is actually malware in disguise.

Finally, protect your web browser. Major browsers (Safari, Chrome, Edge, Firefox, etc.) permit the installation of ad-blocking browser extensions or add-ons. Unfortunately, many unverified extensions harvest data, inject their own adware, or quietly invite secondary malicious extensions to install, slowing down your computer's performance. Choose your browser utilities with care. FIT recommends using one of these vetted, secure, and reputable browser extensions to keep your browsing experience safe.
