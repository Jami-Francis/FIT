Device Market Share

In the United States, the digital landscape is almost split down the middle. Microsoft Windows is still the heavy hitter for traditional computers (desktops and laptops), while Apple's iOS (the iPhone) has secured a comfortable majority in the mobile world. Because smartphones are so essential to daily life, the total number of people in the US using at least one Apple product likely rivals or even exceeds the number of people using Windows.

Computers: The Desktop Battle

When we talk about traditional computers—the kind with a keyboard and mouse—Microsoft Windows is still the standard, largely because it is the default choice for most big business offices and government jobs. However, Apple's Mac computers have a much larger slice of the pie in the US (around 30%) compared to the rest of the world. Interestingly, Linux (a free, open-source alternative favored by tech experts) has recently seen a surge in popularity, finally crossing the 5% mark in the US.

Mobile: The iPhone Majority

The story flips when we look at smartphones. Unlike the rest of the globe where Android is number one, in the US it's the iPhone. With a clear majority - now approaching 60% - it is heavily driven by younger generations and the social pressure of the widespread use of the iMessage app for chatting. Microsoft is essentially non-existent in this category today.

Since a massive portion of the population uses a smartphone as their primary (or only) computer, the raw count of iPhone users (approx. 165 Million) edges out the active Windows user base (approx. 145 Million), especially when accounting for children and elderly users who may own a phone or tablet but not a PC.

The Ecosystem Shift: Why It's Hard to Switch

While the market share numbers tell us what people buy, they don't fully explain why they keep buying it. This is where Apple's "Ecosystem" - often called a "Walled Garden" - comes into play. The definition of a "computer user" has fundamentally shifted. For millions of Americans, the smartphone is their primary computer, and in that specific arena, Apple has effectively won the war of cultural influence.

This creates a unique situation where a person might be forced to use a Windows PC at their office desk from 9-to-5, but they live their entire personal digital life - photos, messages, health data, and music - within Apple's iOS world. This phenomenon is known as "ecosystem lock-in." It simply means that Apple devices are designed to work perfectly with each other, but poorly with anything else. For example, if you have an iPhone, you are statistically very likely to buy AirPods or an Apple Watch. Why? Because those accessories lose significant functionality if you try to pair them with an Android phone.

This strategy has created a formidable barrier to leaving Apple devices. Data shows that iPhone users have a retention rate of over 90%, meaning when they buy a new phone, they almost always buy another iPhone. The divide is even more dramatic among younger users (Gen Z and Millennials), where iPhone adoption is skyrocketing. As these younger users grow up and enter the workforce, they bring their preference for Apple with them, which is why we are seeing Mac usage slowly creep up in professional settings that used to be dominated by Windows.
