Security risks for cellphones and tablets

Security risks on older devices

Your cellphone or tablet may face serious security threats if it is not running the latest software (iOS 26 for Apple or Android 16 for Google phones, Samsung and others). Older devices have at least some security risks, and the older the phone, the greater the danger.

How to check your version:

  • On Apple (iPhone/iPad): Open Settings   and tap on General, then tap About to find the "Software Version."
  • On Android: Open Settings   and scroll to the bottom. Tap on About Phone, then look for Android version.

Typically, more expensive flagship phones receive updates for many years, while budget models may only receive them for one or two. Devices running operating systems more than two years old are highly susceptible to the attacks listed below.

1. Unpatched Vulnerabilities
  • Zero-Day Exploits: These are "holes" in the software known to hackers but not to the phone manufacturer. Because your phone is older, the manufacturer is no longer sending "patches" to plug these holes, leaving your private data exposed.
  • Kernel Vulnerabilities: These are deep-system flaws that allow a hacker to take over the entire device, not just a single app.
2. Modern Malware (2026)
  • Banking Trojans: Threats like "GoldPickaxe" and "GodFather" are designed to steal your money. They work by creating an invisible "overlay" on top of your real banking app to steal your login details. Some can even steal your facial recognition data to bypass security.
  • MFA Bypass: Advanced malware like "MaliBot" can now intercept the one-time text codes (Multi-Factor Authentication) that your bank sends you, allowing hackers to log into your accounts even if they have your password.
3. Advanced Phishing
  • AI-Driven Scams: Hackers now use Artificial Intelligence to write incredibly convincing text messages (Smishing) and emails that look exactly like they came from your bank, UPS, or the government.
  • Quishing (QR Code Scams): Malicious QR codes are used to send you to fake websites. Modern phones have scanners that check if a link is safe, but older phones lack this protection.
4. App & Network Safety
  • Play Integrity Failures: Google and Apple have updated their security "handshakes." If your phone is too old, your banking and finance apps may detect that the device is no longer secure and will stop working entirely to protect your account.
  • Expired Certificates: Older phones have "security certificates" that eventually expire. Once they expire, your phone can no longer tell if a Public Wi-Fi connection (at an airport or hotel) is genuine or a "Man-in-the-Middle" trap.

Recommendation: If your device can no longer update to the latest version of iOS or Android, it is strongly recommended that you stop using it for banking, medical records, or sensitive personal emails.
