Mac and Windows Online Help

Online Help for Windows

Online scanners are cloud-based apps that search for virus/malware infections without installing a full program on the computer. Instead, they will download a single file, which can be deleted once the scan is finished. Note, however, that some scans can take longer than others even with a fast computer and internet connection. Most will also check for rootkits, however if you see that as an option, make sure to use it.

ESETF-SecurePanda Cloud

Online scanners are practical when you are looking for verification that a problem has been fixed or that a computer is infected. These online scanners will remove malware and rootkits. Most importantly, since two antivirus programs should not be installed on a computer at the same time, online scanners are safe to use with your current antivirus program running in the background, including Microsoft's Windows Defender. None of the online scanners above install a full program meant for daily use.

Online Help for Macs

Mac computers are not immune to viruses or malware, it's just that since there are so few of them compared the number of Windows computers, there is much less malware written to attack the Mac OS. Currently on desktop and notebooks, Windows claims a 71% market share while Macs have 16%. If you suspect a threat on your Mac (OS X 10.15 or later), try downloading and running two or three of the products below to remove any infections. To check which macOS or OS X version you are running, click the Apple icon in the top-left corner of your screen and choose About This Mac.

Online Help for Routers

Sometimes security on older retail routers and ISP gateway routers can be poor. Attackers take advantage of lackadaisical ISPs and are able to attack a large number of routers at a time. They typically accomplish this by exploiting unpatched firmware vulnerabilities or logging in using weak, default administrator passwords that were never changed. Once they gain access to the router's internal settings, a common tactic is "DNS hijacking." The attackers silently swap out your trusted, default DNS servers for malicious ones they control. Consequently, when you attempt to visit a legitimate website - like your bank or email provider - the hijacked router secretly redirects you to a fraudulent, look-alike site designed to harvest your passwords and financial data.

Use the links below to check if the Domain Name Server (DNS) address(es) on your router have been compromised. When you use these tools to view your router's current DNS settings, you are looking for the specific IP addresses handling your internet traffic. On a secure router, these addresses will belong either to your Internet Service Provider (ISP) or a reputable public DNS service (such as Google, Cloudflare, or Quad9). If the addresses displayed are unfamiliar, trace back to suspicious locations, or do not match your ISP, it is a strong indicator that your router has been compromised and is actively redirecting your web traffic. This setting can be changed manually if necessary. Domain Name Servers are the Internet's equivalent of a phone book. They maintain a directory of domain names (like https://google.com) and translate them to Internet Protocol (IP) addresses. For example both 142.250.185.142 and 142.250.217.14 will take you straight to Google.
