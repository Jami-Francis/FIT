Mesh Wi-Fi

In a world increasingly reliant on constant and reliable internet access, traditional Wi-Fi setups with a single router often fall short, especially in larger homes or offices with multiple floors or complex layouts. This is where mesh Wi-Fi steps in as a powerful and increasingly popular solution to eliminate dead zones and deliver seamless connectivity throughout a large area. Mesh Wi-Fi technology, initially offered as a key feature in Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac) standards, quickly emerged as a predominant feature in home networking, gaining popularity as a solution for extended Wi-Fi coverage and eliminating dead zones without the complexity of traditional access points (now commonly referred to as "nodes".)

One notable product in this shift was the eero router, marketed in 2016 as the world's first home mesh Wi-Fi system. The initial eero model supported 802.11ac and used multiple nodes to establish an expandable network, providing coverage for larger homes as additional nodes could be purchased and self-installed. Following eero's lead, other major manufacturers like Google (with Google Wifi in late 2016) and Netgear (with their Orbi system in 2018) started offering their own mesh Wi-Fi solutions. These systems, designed for years of service, remain highly effective. With proper maintenance, such as light vacuuming to prevent dust buildup, they can easily support the throughput demands of five to eight users simultaneously.

The Inner Workings of a Mesh Network
Mesh Wi-Fi is a wireless network system that creates a single, expansive network by utilizing multiple interconnected devices called nodes. Instead of relying on a single router to transmit the signal, these nodes work together, effectively blanketing a larger space with a strong and consistent Wi-Fi signal.

The system typically consists of a main router, connected to the modem, and one or more satellite nodes placed strategically to expand coverage. A critical component of a mesh network is the backhaul, which refers to the dedicated connection used for data transmission between the nodes. This is how the nodes communicate with each other and with the main router to extend the network. While this backhaul is often wireless (typically on a separate radio band), it can also be established using wired connections for superior performance.

The nodes seamlessly communicate with each other, constantly optimizing the signal and ensuring devices connect to the strongest available connection as users move throughout the space. This differs from traditional Wi-Fi setups, where devices connect to a single router, and signal strength weakens with distance and obstacles like walls.

The Role of Powerline and MoCA for Backhaul
While wireless backhaul is convenient, it can still be susceptible to interference and signal degradation over long distances or through thick walls. To create a more robust and reliable connection between nodes, many mesh systems support powerline or MoCA (Multimedia over Coax Alliance) for a wired backhaul.

  • Powerline: This technology uses the existing electrical wiring in a building to transmit data. By plugging adapters into power outlets, you can create a high-speed data connection between mesh nodes, effectively turning your home's electrical grid into a network backbone. This eliminates the need to run Ethernet cables through walls and is a simple solution for bridging nodes across different floors or rooms where the Wi-Fi signal would otherwise be weak.

  • MoCA: This uses the existing coaxial cable wiring (the same cables used for cable TV and internet) to create a high-speed Ethernet-like connection. If your home is already wired for cable, MoCA adapters can provide a very fast and stable connection for mesh backhaul, often with higher speeds and lower latency than powerline.

By utilizing these wired backhaul methods, you can ensure that the data split between your nodes remains consistently high-speed, preventing performance bottlenecks and delivering a faster, more reliable Wi-Fi experience, even in areas far from the main router.

Benefits of Mesh Wi-Fi for Large Spaces
Mesh Wi-Fi offers several key benefits for large homes and offices:

  • Expanded Coverage and Elimination of Dead Zones: Multiple nodes distributed throughout the space ensure a strong Wi-Fi signal in every corner.

  • Seamless Connectivity and Improved Roaming: Devices automatically switch to the nearest node with the strongest signal as you move around, preventing connection drops and ensuring a smooth online experience. This is particularly beneficial for activities like video calls and streaming.

  • Consistent Speed and Performance: By distributing the internet signal across multiple nodes and leveraging a robust backhaul, mesh Wi-Fi optimizes network performance and ensures consistent speeds, even in areas further from the main router.

  • Easy Setup and Management: Many mesh systems come with user-friendly mobile apps that simplify the setup process and provide centralized control.

  • Scalability: Expanding coverage is easy by simply adding more nodes to the network as needed.

  • Enhanced Security: Many mesh Wi-Fi systems incorporate modern security features, including advanced encryption like WPA3.

Considerations for Large Homes or Offices
While mesh Wi-Fi offers significant advantages, a few considerations should be kept in mind:

  • Cost: Mesh systems are generally more expensive than traditional routers.

  • Placement: Optimal node placement is crucial for maximizing performance and coverage.

  • Thick Walls and Obstructions: While mesh networks are designed to penetrate walls, extremely thick walls, concrete, or metal structures can still hinder wireless signal transmission, making a wired backhaul solution like MoCA or powerline a critical consideration.

  • Enterprise-grade Solutions: For very large or complex offices with specific security and control requirements, dedicated enterprise wireless solutions might be necessary beyond standard mesh Wi-Fi systems.

It is important to note that Mesh is a Feature, not aÂ Standard, and along those lines here's a breakdown of how mesh technology has evolved across Wi-Fi 5, 6, and 7 standards.

The core Wi-Fi standards (802.11ac/Wi-Fi 5, 802.11ax/Wi-Fi 6, and 802.11be/Wi-Fi 7) define the physical layer of how devices connect and transmit data wirelessly. They dictate things like speed, channel width, and modulation.

Mesh, on the other hand, is a networking architecture. It's a layer of software and hardware that sits on top of the Wi-Fi standard to enable multiple access points to work together as a single, seamless network.

Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac)

  • Does it support mesh? Yes, but it's not a native part of the standard.

  • How it works: Early mesh systems like eero and Google Wifi were introduced during the Wi-Fi 5 era. They used proprietary software and hardware to create a mesh network. This meant you had to buy a complete system from a single manufacturer (e.g., all Google Wifi or all eero units) because their mesh protocols were not compatible with other brands.

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax)

  • Does it support mesh? Yes, and it's much more common.

  • How it works: Wi-Fi 6 builds on the mesh concept and significantly improves it. The Wi-Fi Alliance's "EasyMesh" standard was developed to address the interoperability issues of Wi-Fi 5. EasyMesh is a certification that ensures routers from different manufacturers can theoretically work together in a mesh network. However, most consumers still opt to buy systems from a single brand (e.g., Netgear Orbi, TP-Link Deco, Eero) because a single brand's proprietary software often offers a more cohesive and feature-rich experience.

Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be)

  • Does it support mesh? Yes, it is a key consideration for Wi-Fi 7 devices.

  • How it works: Wi-Fi 7's new technologies are designed to enhance mesh network performance. Features like Multi-Link Operation (MLO) allow devices and mesh nodes to use multiple radio bands (2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and the new 6 GHz) at the same time. This is a game-changer for mesh backhaul, as it can create a much faster and more reliable connection between nodes, reducing latency and increasing overall throughput. While the standard doesn't mandate mesh, the advanced features of Wi-Fi 7 make it perfectly suited for high-performance mesh systems.

You cannot assume that any given router from these standards will support mesh. You must look for a product that is specifically sold as a "mesh Wi-Fi system" or a router that states it can be used to form a mesh network (e.g., with features like ASUS's AiMesh). While mesh is now a common and expected feature, it is still a deliberate design choice by the manufacturer and not a universal requirement of the underlying Wi-Fi standard.
