Infections

Indications that your computer is infected
with malware (malicious software)

Malware is designed to disrupt computer operation, gather sensitive information or gain unauthorized access to a computer systems. While it is usually software, it can also be hidden script or code on a webpage, or it can be installed through a drive-by download (a rogue app installs itself during the installation of another program.) Malware includes computer viruses, ransomware, worms, Trojan horses, spyware, rogue exploits, droppers, backdoors, adware, most rootkits, and other malicious programs.

Spyware is a type of software that is installed on your computer to watch and record your activity. Some types of spyware can record your keystrokes and information that you type into websites or other programs and then use that information for targeted advertising or identity theft. These programs can be installed on your computer in many ways but often they are hidden inside of software such as free games, fake updates to Java, free apps,  illegal music or video downloads, fake antivirus programs, utilities like registry cleaners and fake programs that claim they will make your computer run faster or be more secure.

Here are some signs that your computer might be infected with malware:

• You see new toolbars, links, or favorite places that you did not intentionally add to your web browser.

• Your home page, search engine or mouse pointer has unexpectedly changed.

• You get pop-up ads, particularly for Viagra, or other medications - possibly even when your computer is not connected to the Internet.


• You click on name of specific website (usually from a web search) and a website opens, but the http(s) address is slightly different or you are instead taken to a completely different website.
 
• The computer is running slower that usual. Please note that not all computer performance problems are caused by malware. However, malware can cause a noticeable change in computer speed since malware programs run in the background and can take a great deal of memory as they access the internet as a background process and monopolize the processor.

• Sometimes an infected computer will not show any symptoms, even if malware or a rootkit is running. To help protect your privacy and your computer, we recommend that you keep your Operating System up to date and familiarize ourself with its security settings.   If you buy additional protection make sure "search for rootkits" is enabled.

• If you see a pop-up window disguised as a "official" system notification that claims your computer is infected with malware - do not click on it. If you do, it will usually install more malware and begin to prompt you with ads to remove it by purchasing a fake antivirus program. Literally, in seconds, the scam antivirus program will have purportedly scanned your hard drive and display a report of multiple infections, all fake. These antivirus programs look convincing because they have the look and feel of the respective operating system and mimic real apps.

In fact, if you have never seen how your computer would actually report malware if it did find any, it is worth going to the EICAR (European Institute for Computer Antivirus Research) website to test your current anti-virus software and see what it would look like if your computer were actually infected. This test can be run on any device, including smartphones. Just follow the simple directions on their webpage at https://www.eicar.org/download-anti-malware-testfile   The easiest way to test a device is by going to the bottom of their page and downloading the Zip file which includes a single file EICAR.com Drag the file out of the Zip window and run the EICAR.com (double click on it).  The most common fake alert will happen while web browsing and claim that a virus has been found and that you can clean the system by clicking a link. The safest solution is to close (or force quit) any web browser when the page is unresponsive or it will not close properly. Then restart the browser or the device.

  • On macOS: Click the Apple icon in the upper-left of the screen and choose "Force Quit," then select the web browser and click the "Force Quit" button. The keyboard equivalent to open Force Quit is Command+Option+Esc.

  • On Windows: Right-click the taskbar and choose "Task Manager" (or press Ctrl+Shift+Esc to open it directly). In Task Manager, select the "Processes" or "Applications" tab, click on the web browser, then click "End Task."
