The Gateway Router

A Gateway Router is a single device combining a modem (for DSL, cable, or fiber service) and a router (usually with Wi-Fi) and does not need an additional device to connect to the Internet.

A major benefit is fewer cords and less space than using two separate devices. If your Internet Service Provider (ISP) is charging a monthly fee, itâ€™s often cheaper to buy your own and return the ISP-provided gateway.

The downside is that if the gateway router stops working, you will completely lose your Internet connection. Conversely, if only a separate router fails, you can remove it and connect directly into the modem to get online with at least one computer.

Additionally, gateway routers provided by ISPs are often older, repurposed models. This means the Wi-Fi may be based on older standards (which may be slower than modern 5 GHz Wi-Fi 5 or Wi-Fi 6 over short distances). These older devices often come with basic security settings (with NAT/SPI turned off) that are inferior to a dedicated, high-quality aftermarket router.

Some ISPs (like Zito Media) offer fiber optic connections that replace Gateway Routers with an ONT (Optical Network Terminal), which offer better throughput but often lack additional Ethernet ports or VOIP in a single device.

Remember, there is no problem mixing Windows, Apple, and Android products on any router, including network printers. Feel free to call us for recommendations on how to set up and protect your online connection.
