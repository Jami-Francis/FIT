USB power & data cables

 

USB ports are everywhere. Some USB cables are for power only and others offer both power and data transfers. Since Apple products use proprietary USB ports see their specs on Apple's website: https://support.apple.com/en-us/109523 

The Universal Serial Bus (USB) standard, first released in 1996, was conceived to replace the tangle of proprietary serial and parallel ports with a single, universal connector. The progression from USB 1.0/1.1's initial speed of 12 Mbps ( or 1.5 MBps - MegaBytes per second) focused primarily on convenience, enabling simple peripherals like keyboards and mice to be hot-swappable (aka: Plug & Play). The major leap came with USB 2.0 (480 Mbps) in 2000, which established USB as a legitimate high-speed data interface for external storage and flash drives and to recharge batteries in USB devices.

Data transfer speeds are advertised in Megabits per second (Mbps) primarily because it's the technical standard for measuring the speed of a connection, which fundamentally transfers data as individual bits, but the industry also heavily favors it for marketing since using bits instead of bytes results in a number that is eight times larger (because there are 8 bits in a byte), making the speed look much more impressive to potential customers.

USB standards are designed for backward compatibility to ensure that new ports and hosts can still work with older devices. For example, all ports from USB 3.0 onward maintain a separate set of wires dedicated to the older USB 2.0 protocol, effectively creating a "dual-bus" architecture within the same port. This allows an old USB 2.0 flash drive to plug into a new USB 4 port and function correctly, though its speed will be limited to the slower 480 Mbps rate of the USB 2.0 device itself.

Subsequent versions, starting with USB 3.0 (SuperSpeed) in 2008, ushered in a massive increase in data bandwidth, jumping to 5 Gbps and introducing dedicated lanes for simultaneous two-way data flow. The modern era of USB 3.2 and USB4 has quadrupled speeds up to 40 Gbps and beyond, fundamentally shifting USB's role. These latest standards, reliant on the reversible Type-C connector, integrate Thunderbolt technology and the Power Delivery (PD) protocol, allowing a single cable to handle high-resolution video, high-speed data, and simultaneous power up to 240W.

A USB-C cable can work in a Thunderbolt port, and a Thunderbolt cable can work in a USB-C port - but speeds and functionality will be limited by the less capable device or cable. 

A critical distinction today exists between full-featured data cables and cheaper, "charge-only" power cables. While they may look identical externally (especially USB Type-C), charge-only cables often lack the necessary internal wiring (data lines) to transmit data, video, or enable fast charging protocols like Power Delivery (PD). This means a cheap cable might only charge your cell phone or laptop slowly, or not transfer files at all. Users must closely check the packaging for logos or explicit labeling that indicates speed standards (e.g., "5 Gbps," "USB4 40Gbps") and power capacity (e.g., "PD 100W") to guarantee the cable supports both the required speed and power level for their device.
