Infections

Indications that your computer is infected with malware
(malicious software)

Malware (malicious software) is designed to disrupt computer operation, gather sensitive information, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems. It includes viruses, ransomware, worms, Trojan horses, spyware, and more.

Spyware is a specific type of malware installed to watch and record your activity, sometimes logging keystrokes or information for targeted advertising or identity theft. Malware is often installed through fake updates (Java/Adobe), illegal downloads, or a drive-by download (a rogue app installs itself with another program).

Here are some signs that your computer might be infected with malware:

  • Unexpected Browser Changes:  New toolbars, links, or favorite places appear without your consent.
  • Redirected Web Traffic:  Typing a correct website address takes you to a different, unintended site, or the URL is slightly modified.
  • Modified Settings:  Your home page, default search engine, or mouse pointer has unexpectedly changed.
  • Excessive Pop-ups:  You receive frequent pop-up ads, even when not actively browsing the internet.
  • Severe Performance Loss:  The computer runs significantly slower than usual. Malware runs in the background, consuming memory and monopolizing the processor.
  • Fake System Alerts:  You see a pop-up disguised as an "official" system notification claiming your computer is infected - do not click it! This is a common scam that installs more malware or prompts you to purchase a fake antivirus program.

Important Security Tip:  An infected computer may show no symptoms at all. To protect your privacy, always keep your Operating System (OS) up to date, familiarize yourself with its security settings, and ensure your anti-virus/anti-malware program has rootkit scanning enabled.

To see how your device actually reports malware, you can safely test your current antivirus software using a harmless file from the European Institute for Computer Antivirus Research (EICAR). This test works on Windows, macOS, Linux, Chromebook, iOS, and Android.

In fact, if you have never seen how your computer would actually report malware if it did find any, it is worth going to the EICAR (European Institute for Computer Antivirus Research) website to test your current anti-virus software and see what it would look like if your computer were actually infected. This test can be run on any device, iOS, Windows, Chromebook or Linux, including smartphones or tablets.

On EICAR's webpage, at https://www.eicar.org/download-anti-malware-testfile, follow these steps:

  1. Scroll to the "Download Area" heading.
  2. Choose to download the file EICAR.COM.ZIP.
  3. Locate the downloaded "eicar.com.zip" file. Opening the ZIP file will likely trigger your antivirus app, which should identify a security risk.
  4. Note: The EICAR file is not a true infection; it's a test file. Your antivirus program will treat it like one. Delete the file once the test is complete.

The most common fake alert will happen while web browsing and claim that a virus has been found and that you can clean the system by clicking a link. The safest solution is to close (or force quit) any webpage that looks suspicious. If the webpage is unresponsive follow the directions below.

On macOS:

  • Click the Apple icon in the upper-left of the screen and choose "Force Quit," then select the web browser and click the "Force Quit" button.
  • The keyboard equivalent to open Force Quit is Command+Option+Esc.

On Windows:

  • Right-click the taskbar and choose "Task Manager" (or press Ctrl+Shift+Esc to open it directly).
  • In Task Manager, select the "Processes" or "Applications" tab, click on the web browser, then click "End Task."
